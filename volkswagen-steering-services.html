Steering Basics

The steering of any Volkswagen is a complex system with many parts and issues. Older vehicles can use manual steering whereby the driver must crank the steering wheel directly to move the wheels. You can notice this if you need to pull the wheel extra hard when you're moving at low speed. Newer vehicles are equipped with power steering which allows a light touch on the wheel to move regardless of the speed you're traveling. This type of system is prone to a significant number of failure points and requires expertise very specific to Volkswagens to make sure every point is checked.

Power Steering Components

A power steering system in your Volkswagen consists of the steering wheel and steering column, fluid reservoir, drive belt, control valve, tie rod, and power steering fluid pump. The steering gear causes the turn of the steering wheel to move the wheel axel right or left. The steering fluid pump pressurizes the fluid that is used to actually move the wheel axel. The control valve sets how much power is applied in either direction. And the tie rod keeps the entire system squeezed properly for most efficient power transfer. These components must all rely on one another at all times to create the simple action of turning the wheel.

Power Steering Problems

The most common problem in a power steering system is lack of pressure from the fluid pump. When this happens, you'll notice you still have to turn the steering wheel harder at low speeds. A broken pump typically makes a range of annoying noises, so you will not ignore it. But a pump may simply be operating at too low a speed. The hoses of the system often develop leaks or get air bubbles in the fluid which has much the same effect. You may notice a stain appearing under where you park you car. It's good to check the fluid and get an inspection of the system about once a year, but make sure to take your Volkswagen in right away if there's a serious problem.
