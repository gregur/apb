Turbocharger Basics

A turbocharger greatly increase the power of the engine by providing increased airflow. It draws air into a compressor and compresses the air before sending it to the engine. Thus each cylinder can work with more air. Of course more air means more fuel, so fuel usage will increase with a turbocharger compared to a perfectly-tuned engine. However, it is more often the case that a regular engine is running with too much fuel and not enough air, which results in wasted fuel. Volkswagen turbochargers are very unique in their construction and often require more specialized maintenance than other car makes.

How a Turbocharger Works

A turbocharger is typically powered by the force of the exhaust from the engine. High pressure exhaust gas actually pushes a turbine that works to compress incoming fresh air. Just like the cylinders in the engine, a turbocharger is directly responsive to changes in throttle; the faster the engine spins, the faster the turbocharger compresses. This very high-speed system is of course prone to errors and degradation over time. It's important to make sure a skilled mechanic checks your turbocharger regularly to ensure that it's not on its way down. The turbocharger is the difference between a weak ride and strong one, so it makes sense to put your turbocharger first on the list of check-ups.

The Importance of Oil

Because a turbocharger compressor spins at such high speed, it is important to have good clean oil in the system. Otherwise there will be friction, and when running at speeds up to 150,000rpm, this can produce significant heat and damage the turbocharger. Turbochargers are expensive parts, so it's wise to keep it checked frequently by a skilled mechanic.
